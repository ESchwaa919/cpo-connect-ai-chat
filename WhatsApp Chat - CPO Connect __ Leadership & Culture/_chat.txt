[23/11/2025, 16:25:15] ~ Tochii: The framework we’ve been using centres on building layered reasoning. Instead of jumping from a premise straight to a final conclusion, you structure the argument through intermediate conclusions. Each intermediate conclusion must logically follow from the premises before it, and then becomes a premise for the next step.
[23/11/2025, 16:27:00] ~ Tochii: What this does in practice is force clarity:
	•	You separate facts from assumptions.
	•	You test whether each step actually holds.
	•	And you spot weaknesses much earlier, especially where the logic depends on an unstated belief.

I’ve already started applying it at work. It’s been useful when shaping product strategy discussions especially when multiple stakeholders are making strong claims. Using the step-wise structure helps everyone see why a conclusion follows (or doesn’t), and it reduces a lot of the circular debate. ‎<This message was edited>
[23/11/2025, 16:30:57] ~ Tochii: Example: Instead of saying something like “Airbnb should focus on hosts because it will increase supply” (which is one big jump), the structure forces you to break it into steps:
	•	Travellers want unique and affordable places to stay.
	•	Airbnb can’t offer that without more hosts.
→ So increasing hosts increases differentiated supply.
	•	Differentiated supply is what gives Airbnb an edge over hotels.
→ So growing host supply strengthens their competitive position.
	•	A stronger competitive position drives more sustainable growth.
→ So prioritising host acquisition becomes the logical conclusion.

It’s basically: premises → small conclusions → final conclusion, and each step has to hold up logically.

[04/12/2025, 15:24:11] ~ Sascha Brossmann: First some major caveats, especially with respect to _values_:
1. Following the argument by Buckingham & Goodall in “Nine Lies About Work” (big endorsement, should-read for every manager) there is _no organisational culture_, there are only team/group cultures (sic!). On top of that values are _personal_. Using the set of core values from Schwartz et al (see below) the probability of two individuals sharing the same e.g. five out of 19 is 1 in 11,628. An inquiry for organisational values will hence not produce anything useful, no matter how plausible it may appear. Moreover, if e. g. your accounting or compliance experts operated with the same set of core values as your innovation teams it would be rather disastrous. In short: the concept of org values is utter bullshit. No matter whether you restrict yourself to observations or – much, much worse – commit to the folly of trying to define and mandate org values for governance. 
2. ⁠Culture is observable, values aren’t. You can only infer values from observing strong consistent behavioural preferences and mapping these to an evidence-based set of values (the most solid empirical intercultural model of personal values I am aware of comes from Shalom Schwartz et al. Paper: https://www.zora.uzh.ch/server/api/core/bitstreams/7859b586-355b-468f-8488-446062a83d98/content). Besides that mapping requiring psychological expertise, IMHO you also don’t gain anything from doing so.
3. ⁠I _do_ think that personal values have their place in a) social sciences and b) personal reflection. But absolutely _not_ in the workplace.

Corollary (not theirs but my

distinct org culture.

Here are the baseline culture questions I ask during my due diligence.

Some additional thoughts:

- Too many people ignore orange flags and ignore red flags and the get burnt. Don’t ignore them, they will land every time!
- ⁠Speak to multiple people in the team and look for inconsistencies
- ⁠Glass door is a gold mine. Pick out specific negative things people have written and ask about them - watch for defensiveness.

1. "How would you describe the collaboration between the product team and other departments, such as sales, marketing, and customer support?"
2. "Can you provide an example of a recent decision that was made regarding the product and explain how the decision-making process unfolded within the team and leadership?
